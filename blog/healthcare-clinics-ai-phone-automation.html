Healthcare Clinics: Why 67% of Patient Calls Go Unanswered (And How AI Fixes It)

Your front desk is underwater. Phones ringing. Patients waiting. Insurance forms piling up. And somewhere in that chaos, a new patient just called — and nobody picked up. They will not call back. They will call the clinic down the street instead.

This is not a staffing problem you can hire your way out of. It is a structural problem that requires a different kind of solution.

The maths is brutal. Industry research suggests that medical practices miss a significant percentage of incoming calls. Most of those lost opportunities happen not because of quality of care, but because of simple accessibility: the phone rings, nobody answers, and the patient moves on.

The Hidden Cost of Missed Calls in Healthcare

Here is what happens when a patient cannot get through:

  • They do not leave a voicemail. Studies show 80% of callers will not leave a message — they will just try someone else.
  • They find another provider. In competitive markets, patients have options. One missed call equals one lost patient.
  • Existing patients miss appointments. Confirmation calls that do not go out mean higher no-show rates.
  • Revenue leaks silently. The average patient is worth £2,500-5,000 per year. How many are you losing to hold music?

A single missed call might cost you £150 in appointment revenue. But that patient might have stayed with your practice for 10 years. The real cost? Potentially £25,000 or more.

Why Traditional Solutions Do Not Work

Hiring More Staff

At £25,000-35,000 per year per receptionist (plus benefits, training, sick days), you would need 2-3 additional hires to cover all hours. That is £75,000 or more annually — and you still will not have 24/7 coverage.

Answering Services

Call centres can take messages, but they cannot book appointments in your system, check availability, or handle complex patient questions. Plus, patients know they are not talking to your practice.

Better Phone Systems

Call routing and queues help — but they do not solve the fundamental problem: someone still needs to answer.
